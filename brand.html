02 · The mark

Two circles. One pulse.

The mark is a lockup of two perfect circles — one open ring, one solid disc — separated by a crescent carved out of the disc along the ring's outer edge. The ring is the prospect; unknown, untouched. The disc is the response; full, engaged. The carved negative space between them is the pulse — the signal moving outward.

Geometric, deliberate, whole.

Both circles sit at the same radius. The disc is anchored at the grid centre; the ring hangs off to the left, overlapping 10% of the disc's width. The crescent gap is carved by a 48-unit stroke mask so the disc and ring read as one composed shape, not two floating objects.

    Reads at any sizeNo fine detail breaks at 16px; no empty space floods at 128px.
  • 02

  • 03
    Never flipped or rotatedThe ring always sits on the left of the disc. The geometry is the meaning.
03 · Wordmark

Instrument Serif pairs the geometry.

The circle mark is pure geometry; the wordmark should echo that, not fight it. Playfair's high-contrast, italic-leaning display feel pulled toward editorial. Instrument Serif — a modern cut with near-circular bowls on O, D, and Q — reads as a natural twin to the mark. Its subtle contrast and sharp terminals give the lockup bite without ornament.

05 · Clear space · sizing

06 · Colour

Ink on cream, ember when it matters.

The default surface is cream on white; the default mark is ink. Ember (the brand orange) is reserved for things that carry meaning — italics, active states, status indicators, the one big CTA on a page. Violet, blue, and green are data-layer colours, used in dashboards to separate categories of information.

07 · Typography system

A two-family scale.

Instrument Serif is the display voice — headlines, hero statements, the one italic accent on a page. Inter runs the working layer — UI labels, tables, body copy, metadata. Never mix them in the same line; let the scale switch guide the eye from shape to substance.

08 · Voice

Direct. Quiet. Earned.

Outpulse writes the way a senior salesperson emails — short sentences, zero filler, signals in the first line. We don't shout, we don't use emoji, we don't promise. We describe what we do plainly and let results carry the pitch.

Confident, not loud.

Is
  • Specific numbers over superlatives
  • Short sentences · one idea at a time
  • Plain words · sentence case
  • Honest about trade-offs
Isn't
  • "Revolutionary AI-powered platform"
  • Three-clause paragraphs
  • ALL CAPS or exclamation points
  • Vague promises of scale
"Sarah, saw you just closed Series C. Worth a quick chat?"

Helpful, not hungry.

Is
  • Explains why the email is relevant
  • Offers 15 minutes, not a demo
  • Tells the reader the ask in line one
  • Says "if this isn't right, say so"
Isn't
  • "Circling back" · "just wanted to"
  • Bait-and-switch subject lines
  • Pretending the email isn't outbound
  • Guilt-tripping follow-ups
"If Outpulse isn't right for Vercel, I'll say so in five minutes."
09 · Do's and don'ts

Keep the mark honest.

Six small rules that cover 95% of how the logotype gets abused.

Outpulse
Do
Use Instrument Serif for the wordmark
It's the approved pairing. Sizes and weights set in the system above.
Outpulse
Don't
Never substitute the typeface
System serifs or Comic Sans break the composition. If Instrument Serif isn't available, ship Georgia and file a ticket.
Outpulse
Do
Ember lockup, sparingly
Full-ember lockup is reserved for brand moments — launches, partnerships. Not a default.
Outpulse
Don't
Never sit the lockup on a gradient
Mark needs flat ground. Solid ink, cream, paper, or ember — that's it.
Do
Mark-only at 16–48 px
Favicons, avatars, inline glyphs — drop the wordmark. Mark reads cleanly on its own.
Outpulse
Don't
Never rotate or skew the mark
The ring sits on the left of the disc. Always. Rotating the pair breaks the meaning.
